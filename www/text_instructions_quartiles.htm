Instructions

What types of questions will we ask you?

We will ask you to estimate some of our model inputs, such as 30-day mortality and the average hospital length of stay in specific patient populations. We are interested in your uncertainty around these values.

What do we mean by uncertainty?

Uncertainty refers to how (un)sure you are about an answer to a question. For example, the average length of stay after a myocardial infarction (MI) can be 14 days, but it could also be as low as 9 days or as high as 17. This is uncertainty! It describes a range of plausible answers to a question.

This is different to thinking about variation between individual patients. For example, one patient may be discharged 3 days after an MI, while another may need to stay in hospital for over a month. This isn't uncertainty because it refers to variation between patients, instead of a range of plausible values for the same type of or average patient.

How will you express your uncertainty?

In order to express your uncertainty, first you will be asked to give a range of possible values. For example, if we are asking you for the average hospital length of stay following an MI, the plausible range represents

- Your lowest plausible length of stay (minimum) - a value such that you believe that there is a 1% probability that the length of stay is less than that value.

- Your highest plausible length of stay (maximum) - a value, such that you believe that there is a 1% probability that the length of stay is more than that value.

- So you believe that there is 98% probability between the lowest and the highest values.

Test your range by imagining that somebody gives a value that is outside your plausible range. Your reaction should be that the person has misunderstood or misremembered, i.e. you are very confident that you have chosen the right range!

Let's say that the average length of stay is unlikely to be less than 9, or longer than 17 days.

 

 

After giving your plausible range, you will then be required to provide your midpoint value, also referred to as the median (M) or second quartile (Q2).

The median represents the value that is equally likely to be between your lower limit and median value, or median value and upper limit. It is the value separating the lower half of the values from the higher half.

For example, if your median value in the example above is 12 days, that implies that you believe that hospital length of stay following an MI equally likely to be less than 12 days, as it is to be greater than 12 days.

Your median isn't necessarily half way between your upper and lower limits - i.e. in this example, it doesn't have to be 13 (mid point between 9 and 17 days).

 

 

Next, you will be asked for the lower and upper quartile.

The lower quartile is a value between the lower end of your range and your median that divides the values lower than the median into two halves, so that the quantity of interest is equally likely to be less than the lower quartile and between the lower quartile and the median. The lower quartile is also known as the first quartile (Q1).

The upper quartile is a value between your median and the higher end of your range that divides the values greater than the median into two halves, so that the quantity of interest is equally likely to be between the median and the upper quartile, and greater than the upper quartile. The upper quartile is also called the third quartile (Q3).

Quartiles divide your range into four equally likely intervals. For example, if your median is 13 days and your upper and lower quartiles are 11 and 14 days, respectively, this indicates that the average hospital length of stay following an MI is equally likely to be less than 11 days, as it is to be between 11 and 12 days, between 12 and 14 days, and greater than 14 days.

Your quartiles are not necessarily half way between your range and the median. In fact, quartiles tend to be closer to the median than your range, indicating that the value is more likely to be closer to the median then the limits of your range.
